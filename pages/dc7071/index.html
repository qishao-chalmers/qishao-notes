# 🤖 [0] Summary of Reinforcement Learning Concepts

This document summarizes the core definitions and differences for key concepts in policy gradient methods, as discussed in the context of the paper, "Stabilizing Reinforcement Learning with LLMs".

# 1. On-Policy vs. Off-Policy Training

These terms define the relationship between the policy generating the data and the policy being optimized.

Feature On-Policy Training Off-Policy Training
Definition The policy sampling the responses ($\mu_{\theta_{old}}$) is the same as the policy being optimized ($\pi_{\theta}$). The policy sampling the responses ($\mu_{\theta_{old}}$) is different from the policy being optimized ($\pi_{\theta}$).
Policy Staleness Minimized, as the policies are theoretically identical (omitting training-inference discrepancy). Increased, typically to accelerate training by reusing data for multiple gradient updates.
Sample Efficiency Lower. Requires generating a new batch of data for every (or very few) update steps. Higher. Allows for reusing old data for multiple gradient steps.
LLM Stabilization Requires IS Correction to address the training-inference discrepancy. Requires Clipping and Routing Replay (R2/R3) to safely mitigate the instability caused by policy staleness.
Example Algorithms REINFORCE, traditional GRPO (when data is used once). Modern PPO, GRPO/MiniRL when using large batch sizes split into mini-batches.

# 2. REINFORCE vs. PPO

These are two of the most significant algorithms in the policy gradient family.

# A. REINFORCE (The Foundation)

  • Policy Type: On-Policy.
  • Mechanism: It is the foundational token-level objective used to optimize the policy directly by moving the probability of actions up based on the final Monte Carlo reward (return, $G_t$).
  • Drawback: Suffers from high variance in its gradient estimates, leading to unstable and slow training. The paper's MiniRL baseline is a modified REINFORCE objective.

# B. PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)

  • Policy Type: On-Policy (Designed for safe data reuse).
  • Mechanism: PPO is an Actor-Critic method. It replaces the noisy Monte Carlo return ($G_t$) with a low-variance Advantage Estimate ($A_t$) calculated using a value network (Critic).
  • Key Stability Feature: It uses a clipped surrogate objective to explicitly limit how far the new policy can deviate from the old policy in a single step. This ensures stability and allows for safe data reuse.

# C. Comparison

Feature REINFORCE PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)
Stability Low stability (High variance in $G_t$). High stability (Low variance in $A_t$ and uses clipping).
Value Function (Critic) No Critic (Pure Policy Gradient). Yes, uses a Critic to calculate the Advantage ($A_t$).
Data Usage Sample Inefficient (Data used once). Sample Efficient (Data reused safely due to clipping).

# 3. GRPO (Group Policy Optimization)

  • Framework: GRPO operates within a policy optimization framework.
  • Classification: It is a policy gradient algorithm often used in an on-policy setup.
  • Paper's Finding of 1[qwen] paper The paper argues that GRPO's common objective is prone to failure because it includes length normalization (which introduces bias) and omits the crucial training-inference IS correction.

# 1 [qwen] Stabilizing RL with LLMs: Formulation and Practices

This paper proposes a theoretical formulation and provides empirical practices for stabilizing Reinforcement Learning (RL) with Large Language Models (LLMs). The central challenge addressed is the mismatch between optimizing a sequence-level reward (score for the complete response) and using a token-level objective (updates based on individual tokens).

# 1. Core Theoretical Insight

The paper posits that the token-level objective ($\mathcal{J}^{token}$) can be used as a first-order approximation of the true sequence-level objective ($\mathcal{J}^{seq}$). This approximation is valid only when two specific discrepancies are minimized:

  1. Training-Inference Discrepancy: The numerical difference between the training engine ($\pi_{\theta_{old}}$) and the inference engine ($\mu_{\theta_{old}}$).
  2. Policy Staleness: The difference between the data-sampling policy ($\mu_{\theta_{old}}$) and the policy being optimized ($\pi_{\theta}$).

# 2. Key Stabilization Techniques (Including Your Questions)

The paper explains how crucial stabilization techniques work by minimizing these discrepancies:

Technique Discrepancy Addressed Role in MoE Models
Importance Sampling (IS) Correction Training-Inference Discrepancy. The factor $\frac{\pi_{\theta_{old}}}{\mu_{\theta_{old}}}$ corrects for numerical differences between the training and inference engines, despite having the same parameters. Omitting this causes rapid training collapse.
Clipping Policy Staleness. Prevents aggressive policy updates, thereby restraining policy staleness.
Routing Replay (R2/R3) Training-Inference Discrepancy (R3) & Policy Staleness (R2/R3). Essential for MoE models. It stabilizes training by fixing the routed experts during policy optimization.

# 3. Empirical Findings (On-Policy vs. Off-Policy)

The experiments use MiniRL, a baseline based on the REINFORCE objective but with IS correction and clipping.

  • On-Policy Training (Low Staleness): The basic policy gradient algorithm with IS correction (MiniRL) is the most stable and performs best.
  • Off-Policy Training (High Staleness): Introducing off-policy updates (data reuse for multiple steps) requires both clipping and Routing Replay (R2/R3) to achieve stability and prevent collapse. The degree of off-policiness determines whether R2 or R3 performs better.

# 4. Algorithm Context (GRPO)

  • GRPO (Group Policy Optimization) is a policy gradient algorithm with a token-level optimization framework.
  • The paper argues that GRPO's common formulation—which includes length normalization and omits the training-inference IS correction—invalidates the first-order approximation, leading to a biased objective and suboptimal performance.

# 5. Final Conclusion

Stable training plays a decisive role in successfully scaling RL. Once training is stabilized with the proper techniques, the model consistently converges to comparable final performance, regardless of the initial cold-start data.

Technique Problem Addressed Contribution to Training
1. Clip-Higher Entropy collapse: The policy's entropy decreases too quickly, limiting exploration. Promotes diversity and exploration by decoupling the clipping range: $1-\epsilon_{low}$ and $1+\epsilon_{high}$. It increases the upper bound ($\epsilon_{high}=0.28$) to allow low-probability 'exploration' tokens more room to increase their probability.
2. Dynamic Sampling Zero-gradient problem: If all sampled outputs for a prompt are correct (or all incorrect and receive the same reward), the advantage is zero, leading to zero policy gradients and degrading sample efficiency. Improves training efficiency and stability by over-sampling and filtering out prompts where all outputs have accuracy equal to 1 or 0. This ensures a consistent number of prompts with "effective gradients" in each batch.
3. Token-Level Policy Gradient Loss Disproportionate weighting: Sample-level loss in GRPO assigns equal weight to all samples, making tokens in longer, high-quality responses contribute less, and failing to effectively penalize low-quality (e.g., gibberish) patterns in long samples. Crucial in long-CoT RL scenarios. It calculates the loss at the token level, ensuring longer, reasoning-intensive sequences have a proportional influence on the gradient update and preventing unhealthy increases in response length and entropy.
4. Overlong Reward Shaping Reward noise: Truncated (overlong) samples are often penalized, which can confuse the model by penalizing a sound reasoning process just for its excessive length. Reduces reward noise and stabilizes training. Uses a Soft Overlong Punishment mechanism to assign a length-aware penalty only within a specified punishment interval (i.e., between $L_{max}-L_{cache}$ and $L_{max}$ tokens), signaling the model to avoid excessive length without harshly penalizing potentially valid long responses.

# 3. [557 2025] SFT Memorizes, RL Generalizes: A Comparative Study of Foundation Model Post-training 👍 👍 👍

# 1. Motivation and Problem Statement

The paper examines how post-training strategies—Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL)—affect generalization and memorization in foundation models, focusing on rule-based reasoning tasks and visual reasoning tasks. The key questions are:

  • Does SFT or RL primarily memorize the training data?
  • Does either approach help models generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) variants in both textual and visual domains? The authors argue that understanding this distinction is crucial for building robust AI systems that can reason beyond memorized data.

# 2. Experimental Setup

The authors designed two benchmark environments:

  • GeneralPoints – A card game testing arithmetic reasoning.
    • Input: 4 cards (text or image).
    • Task: Compute a target number (default 24) using each card exactly once.
    • Variants:
      • Rule variations: face cards ‘J’, ‘Q’, ‘K’ mapped differently (10 vs 11,12,13) to test textual rule generalization.
      • Visual variations: different card colors to test visual OOD generalization.
      • Reward structure: Correct equations +10, wrong calculation -1 to -5, illegal numbers -5, visual recognition penalty -1.5 (p. 4, Fig. 2).
  • V-IRL – A real-world navigation environment testing spatial reasoning.
    • Input: visual-linguistic observation of streets and landmarks.
    • Task: Navigate to a destination using instructions.
    • Variants:
      • Absolute vs Relative orientation action spaces.
      • Visual variations by changing city environments (New York vs other cities) to test visual OOD generalization (p. 4-5, Fig. 4).

Both environments allow testing SFT vs RL under controlled in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios.

# 3. Key Methodology

# 3.1 RL with Sequential Revision

Multi-turn RL setup: At each step, the model generates an output which is verified by a verifier module. Sequential revision: Future prompts include all previous outputs and verifier feedback (v_in_t = concatenation of previous outputs & verifier info) (p. 3-4, Fig. 2-3). Reward: Outcome-based, leveraging verifier feedback to encourage correct reasoning.

# 3.2 SFT

SFT uses optimal prompt-response pairs (without iterative verification) to fine-tune the model. Acts as a format teacher to stabilize outputs for subsequent RL training.

# 3.3 Evaluation Metrics

Success rate for GeneralPoints (correct formula generation) and V-IRL (per-step accuracy along the trajectory). OOD vs ID evaluation to distinguish memorization from generalization. Compute metrics: GFLOPs for scaling experiments and verifying compute efficiency (p. 18).

# 4. Main Findings

# 4.1 Memorization vs Generalization

  • SFT tends to memorize training rules and fails on OOD tasks.
  • RL generalizes across both textual rules and visual variations.
  • Quantitative examples (p. 6-7, Fig. 6-7):
    • GP-L OOD success: SFT drops from 11.5% → 3.4%; RL improves 11.5% → 15%.
    • V-IRL-VL OOD: SFT drops 35.7% → 2.5%; RL rises 35.7% → 45%. RL also boosts visual recognition accuracy, improving generalization in VLMs (Fig. 8, p. 8).

# 4.2 Role of SFT in RL Training

  • RL cannot be effectively trained from scratch on uninitialized models.
  • SFT is necessary for stabilizing output formats, enabling RL to learn properly (Fig. 9, p. 7; failure examples on p. 20-21).

# 4.3 Scaling Effects

  • Increasing inference-time verification steps (multi-turn RL) improves OOD performance:
  • More verification steps → more generalization gains (p. 8, Fig. 10).
  • Scaling post-training compute enhances RL’s ability to generalize but may degrade SFT performance in visual recognition (p. 9, Fig. 8).

# 4.4 Failure Modes

  • SFT struggles with visual OOD tasks (GP-VL and V-IRL-VL).
  • RL cannot recover from overfitted SFT checkpoints, showing limitations when the initialization is too biased (p. 9, Fig. 19).
  • Without SFT, RL fails to follow instructions (p. 7, Fig. 20-21).

# 5. Conclusion

RL generalizes; SFT memorizes.

  • RL learns rules transferable to unseen environments.
  • SFT mainly stabilizes format and output structure.
  • SFT is a prerequisite for effective RL in multi-turn reasoning tasks. Visual capabilities improve as a by-product of RL.

Scalability matters:

  • Increasing verification steps and RL compute boosts generalization.
  • Scaling SFT alone can harm visual recognition.

Limitations:

  • RL’s effectiveness is sensitive to SFT initialization quality.
  • Generalization is task-dependent; extreme corner cases are still challenging.

# 6. Takeaways for Foundation Model Training

  • Use SFT to prime the model, then RL to teach generalizable reasoning.
  • For multi-modal models, RL not only helps reasoning but also enhances visual perception.
  • Evaluation should consider both rule-based and visual OOD tasks for a holistic view of generalization.
